Extreme Weather and Trade Costs: Evidence from Mississippi River Drought

Minseong Kang, and Seungki Lee.

Revise and Resubmit at American Journal of Agricultural EconomicsGage Data & Code

Abstract

This paper measures the economic costs of Mississippi River droughts by evaluating changes in U.S. state-level trade for grain-related products, based on a partial-equilibrium gravity model embedded with river segment-specific trade-cost shocks. To identify the trade elasticity to barge rates, we construct a novel index capturing localized hydrological shocks from daily river-level data across all gauges within the barge trading network. We find that disruptions sharply raise barge rates, reducing barge flows while forcing modal substitution in non-barge flows, particularly in barge-dependent upriver states. Counterfactuals reveal these disruptions cost roughly $467 billion (4.2%) in aggregate grain trade, demonstrating the economic value of investing in inland waterway resilience.

Food Inflation Passed-through from Agricultural Imports in a Small Open Economy

Minseong Kang, and Seungki Lee.

American Journal of Agricultural Economics, 2026

Abstract

This paper develops a new framework to measure cost pass-through in a small open economy by estimating firm markup responses to imported crop price shocks. We theoretically show that pass-through varies with firms’ reliance on imported inputs and their demand curvature, generating heterogeneous inflationary effects. For empirical implementation, using financial statements from 10,911 food manufacturers (2000–2021), we document rising markup polarization and significant heterogeneity in markup elasticities across sectors and firm sizes under strategic pricing. Our pass-through measures reveal substantial underlying variation despite stable aggregate patterns; most notably, rising pass-through among firms less dependent on foreign crops. These findings underscore how strategic pricing and firm-specific characteristics shape equilibrium pass-through.

Uneven State-level Exposure to Potential U.S. Tariff Disputes with Canada and Mexico

Minseong Kang, Seungki Lee, Ian Sheldon, and Wendong Zhang.

Choices, 2026Media Coverage

Abstract

Using 2024 Freight Analysis Framework data, we assess US states’ uneven exposure potential to 2025 tariff shocks with Canada and Mexico. Results highlight a dual squeeze: Net-exporting states risk income losses from reduced foreign demand, while import-dependent states face rising food costs. Border states are particularly vulnerable.

Market Power and Cost-efficiency Effects: Broiler Packing Industry in South Korea

Minseong Kang, and Byeong-il Ahn.

Agribusiness, 2023

Abstract

The effects of industrial concentration on markups in the meatpacking industry remain the subject of ongoing global debate. Assuming a generalized Leontief cost structure for meatpackers, this paper develops a framework that decomposes the effect of concentration on markups into two opposing forces: a market-power effect and a cost-efficiency effect. Using firm-level broiler slaughtering records from South Korea (2017–2022), we find that rising concentration has gradually reduced packers' markups. This decline is driven primarily by the cost-efficiency effect, despite a stable market-power effect throughout the study period.

Renewable Diesel Expansion and the Transition Toward Market Resilience in Soybeans

Minseong Kang, and Seungki Lee.

Abstract

The U.S. renewable diesel boom sharply expanded domestic soybean crushing, drawing a growing share of the crop into local processing and away from barge-borne exports down the Mississippi River. We ask whether this supply chain reorganization buffered inland soybean prices against river droughts. We estimate a storage-based structural VAR that allows crush activity to respond endogenously to market conditions, and identify river-disruption shocks using theory-based elasticity bounds. Holding crush activity at its pre-boom level, in the counterfactual analysis, reveals lower inland spot prices by 5.5% on average over 2021–2024. The results indicate that renewable diesel expansion changed not only the level of soybean demand, but also the price consequences of recurrent transportation shocks.

Dynamic Trade Reallocation under Inland Waterway Disruptions: Evidence from U.S. Port-level Grain Exports

Minseong Kang, and Seungki Lee.

Abstract

Droughts have increasingly disrupted the Mississippi River system, which carries roughly half of all U.S. grain exports. Using monthly port-level data for 178 ports over 2013–2024, this study examines how the U.S. port network dynamically reallocates export shipments when their vital domestic shipping lane is disrupted by droughts. The dynamic causal impact of river disruption on port-level trade flows are estimated within a local-projection IV framework, exploiting localized variation across 44 river gages as a Bartik shift-share instrument for barge rates. We find that the U.S. port network buffers waterway disruptions only in the short run. Non-Gulf ports absorb nearly the entire Gulf shortfalls within the first six months, but these gains fade over the 12-month horizon, leaving a clear cumulative shortfall. Substitution speed and durability depend on the geometry of the alternative and seasonal contractual rigidity.
